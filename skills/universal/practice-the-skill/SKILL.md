# Practice The Skill

Purpose: turn a learning goal into repeatable practice that improves ability.

## Rules

- Practice the target ability, not just content about the ability.
- Start from the learner's current level.
- Use feedback quickly.
- Keep drills short enough to repeat.
- Track evidence of improvement.
- Treat practice cycles as learning releases: ability, proof, correction, and next progression.
- Prefer real use over passive review once basics are in place.

## Diagnose

Establish:

- target ability
- current level
- why it matters
- available time and energy
- materials, tools, teachers, communities, or references
- feedback source
- likely friction
- evidence of competence
- practice log, repo, notebook, recordings, or other durable record

## Design The Loop

Create a loop with:

1. Focus: one ability to train.
2. Input: example, lesson, reference, pattern, conversation, demo, or source material.
3. Attempt: exercise, recall, build, speak, write, solve, perform, or produce.
4. Feedback: correction, comparison, test, review, recording, score, or reflection.
5. Repeat: same skill with variation.
6. Review: spaced recall or next-session warmup.
7. Progression: when to make it harder.

## Learning Releases

For each meaningful cycle, produce proof of ability rather than only a time log. Save the durable parts: corrected mistakes, examples, small projects, recordings, transcripts, flashcards, explanations, or reflections.

Use Git when practice creates text, code, datasets, notes, prompts, exercises, or reusable artifacts. Use a notebook, app, folder, or private log when that better fits the skill. Keep raw attempts if they help review; archive or discard noise when it creates friction.

## Domain Notes

For languages, balance listening, speaking, reading, writing, vocabulary, grammar in context, pronunciation, and real conversation.

For programming or analytics, practice by building, querying, debugging, explaining, and validating, not only reading docs.

For writing or creative work, practice with constraints, drafts, imitation, revision, feedback, and publication or sharing when useful.

For games or tools, practice by making tiny playable or usable artifacts and testing the core experience early.
