# Shape The Brief

Purpose: convert decisions and context into an execution-ready brief.

## Rules

- Do not re-interview unless a missing answer would change the work.
- Use existing context, decisions, artifacts, data, notes, drafts, and examples.
- Preserve decision IDs.
- Separate goals from non-goals.
- Define evidence before proposing work.
- Give the brief a release shape: artifact, evidence, completion record, and next version.
- Keep the brief short enough to use.

## Process

1. Identify the scope and source of truth.
2. Read enough context to avoid invention.
3. Map decisions to outcomes and behavior.
4. Identify the evidence of progress or completion.
5. Identify where the artifact, learning proof, report, draft, or release record should live.
6. Name the smallest valuable first step.
6. Publish or return the brief in the agreed location.

## Format

```md
# Brief: <endeavor>

## Problem
<what needs attention>

## Goal
<smallest valuable outcome>

## Non-goals
- <excluded work>

## Decisions
- DEC-001: <short trace>

## Outcome
<what will be true when this is done>

## Experience / Process
<observable behavior, workflow, practice, reading path, creative shape, routine, or interaction>

## Materials and Interfaces
<code, data, tools, spaces, documents, references, people, systems, APIs, datasets, assets, or habits affected>

## Artifact / Release Record
<what durable artifact changes, where it lives, and whether it should be committed, logged, published, or left as scratch>

## Evidence
<test, demo, metric, draft, example, recording, quiz, reconciliation, review, checklist, sign-off, or lived proof>

## First Useful Step
<smallest step that creates visible progress>

## Open Questions
Only blockers that can change the work.
```

## Domain Notes

For software, include user-visible behavior, interfaces, data, permissions, and checks.

For analytics, include metric definitions, data sources, validation, audience, and publication.

For books or creative work, include audience, promise, form, tone, references, draft target, and feedback.

For games, include player fantasy, core loop, platform, constraints, prototype evidence, and fun test.

For learning, include target ability, current level, practice loop, feedback, proof of competence, and the learning log or artifact to keep.

## Done

Return:

- brief location or full brief
- artifact, versioning, or completion-record location
- blockers or risks
- recommended next skill, usually `carve-the-path` or `carry-the-step`
